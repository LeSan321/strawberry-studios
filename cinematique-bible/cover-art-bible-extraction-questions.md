# Cover Art Visual Grammar Bible — Extraction Questions

These questions are designed to pull structured, production-usable knowledge from the specialist AI. Each chapter builds on the foundation document already established (psychology, physics, narrative architecture). The goal is content that can be translated directly into prompt vocabulary and decision frameworks for AI image generation.

Present each chapter heading and its questions as a single exchange. Allow the AI to respond fully before moving to the next chapter. If a response introduces a concept that needs deeper specification (e.g., a technique name without a concrete example), follow up with: *"Can you give me a specific visual example of that and the prompt language that would produce it?"*

---

## Chapter 2 — The Collision Grammar: Building Impossible Scenes That Feel Real

*Context to give the AI before asking:*
> "The foundation document covered the universal grammar of why cover art works. Now I want to go deeper into a specific challenge: the best album covers often combine elements that don't belong in the same physical world — a chrome horse on a salt flat, a figure standing in a burning library, a city skyline reflected in a teardrop. These images feel emotionally coherent even though they are physically impossible. I want to understand the structural rules that make a visual collision feel intentional and resonant rather than random and chaotic."

**Questions:**

1. What are the structural rules that determine whether a visual collision between elements from different worlds feels coherent versus arbitrary? Is there a hierarchy of decisions — what gets decided first, second, third?

2. When you combine elements from incompatible physical environments (organic and industrial, natural and digital, ancient and futuristic), what are the specific visual physics that must remain consistent across all elements to make the composite feel like a single scene rather than a collage? Please be specific — I want to know exactly which properties must match and which can deliberately clash.

3. Scale relationships between objects from different worlds seem critical. What are the rules? When does making something unexpectedly large or small create emotional power, and when does it just look wrong?

4. Light source consistency in impossible scenes — if the scene has no physically possible single light source, how do you establish a convincing lighting logic that the viewer accepts? What are the specific techniques for making composite lighting feel unified?

5. What is the role of atmospheric depth (haze, fog, aerial perspective) in making a dreamscape feel like a real environment rather than a flat composite? What are the specific visual cues that signal "this is a real space with real depth" even when the space is impossible?

6. Are there specific categories of visual collision that reliably produce emotional resonance — combinations that work across genres and audiences? And are there categories that reliably fail or feel juvenile regardless of execution?

## Chapter 3 — The Metaphor Translation Method: From Emotional State to Physical Scene

*Context to give the AI before asking:*
> "The hardest part of cover art design is the translation step — moving from what a song is *about* emotionally to a specific visual scene that carries that feeling. The obvious approach is to illustrate the lyrics literally, but that almost always produces generic results. The images that actually resonate seem to find the physical equivalent of the emotional state rather than the subject matter. I want to understand the method for making that translation — not a list of symbols, but a repeatable process."

**Questions:**

1. What is the step-by-step process for translating an abstract emotional state into a specific physical scene? Walk me through it as if you were doing it in real time for a song about the feeling of being on the edge of a major life change — not the event itself, but the suspended moment just before.

2. There seems to be a difference between images that *illustrate* a feeling and images that *embody* it. What is that difference structurally? What makes an image feel like it *is* the emotion rather than a picture of something related to it?

3. How do you find the symbolic anchor for a song when the lyrics are abstract or metaphorical rather than narrative? What is the process for identifying which single object, figure, or environment can carry the full emotional weight of the piece?

4. Juxtaposition seems to be one of the most powerful tools in cover art — placing two things together that create meaning neither has alone. What are the structural rules for productive juxtaposition? How do you know when two elements will generate meaning versus just confusion?

5. Some songs have a very clear emotional temperature (grief, rage, euphoria) and others have a more complex or contradictory emotional state (nostalgia mixed with dread, joy mixed with loss). How does the visual approach differ for emotionally complex or contradictory material? What specific techniques handle emotional ambiguity without collapsing into visual noise?

6. Genre creates strong visual expectations — country has one visual grammar, electronic has another, jazz has another. When you want to honor the genre's visual language while also making something unexpected, what is the method? How do you find the element that is familiar enough to be legible but strange enough to be memorable?

## Chapter 4 — Rendering Physics: Making the Dream Convincing

*Context to give the AI before asking:*
> "The final layer I need to understand is the rendering physics — the specific technical properties that make an AI-generated image feel photographically real rather than plastic or synthetic. I already have a deep knowledge base on cinematographic light physics (Kelvin temperatures, shadow ratios, fabric behavior under specific light sources). I want to understand how those same principles apply to static cover art, and what additional physics are specific to the cover art context — particularly for impossible or surreal scenes."

**Questions:**

1. What are the specific light physics properties that most reliably signal "this is a real photograph" to the human visual system, even when the scene being depicted is impossible? I want the specific technical parameters — color temperature, shadow softness, highlight rolloff, atmospheric scatter — not general principles.

2. Surface physics: different materials respond to light in fundamentally different ways. For cover art that combines materials from different worlds (chrome, organic skin, weathered wood, liquid, fabric, stone), what are the specific rendering properties of each material that must be correct for the composite to feel real? What does the human eye catch first when a material is rendered incorrectly?

3. Atmospheric depth is one of the most powerful tools for making a composite feel like a real environment. What are the specific visual properties of atmospheric haze, aerial perspective, and depth-of-field that signal real spatial depth? What are the most common mistakes in AI-generated images that break the illusion of depth?

4. Color grading in film and photography creates emotional coherence across a scene — everything shares a color logic even when the sources are different. How does this translate to cover art? What are the specific color grading approaches that unify a composite image and make it feel like it was captured rather than assembled?

5. The human figure in cover art has specific physics challenges — skin, hair, clothing, and posture all have strong perceptual expectations. What are the most critical rendering properties to get right for a human figure to feel real rather than synthetic? What are the specific failure modes that make AI-generated figures feel uncanny?

6. Fabric and clothing physics seem to be a particular area of expertise. For cover art that includes figures, what are the specific properties of different fabric types (natural fibers, synthetics, leather, structured vs. unstructured garments) that must be rendered correctly? How does fabric behavior under different light sources (hard directional light vs. soft diffused light vs. backlight) differ, and what are the prompt-level descriptions that produce accurate fabric physics?

## Follow-Up Protocol

After each chapter response, if the AI introduces a specific technique, material property, or visual principle by name, ask: *"Can you give me the exact prompt language — the specific words and phrases — that would instruct an AI image generator to apply that technique correctly?"*

This converts the knowledge from descriptive to operational — from understanding *why* something works to knowing *what to type* to produce it.
